Diatonic Polychords

Two triads, two hands · scale-degree notation · lever & pedal harp

A polychord is two chords played simultaneously — one in each hand. Stacking diatonic triads produces rich extended sonorities (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) without reaching for chromatic notes. This page catalogs useful diatonic polychord pairings, the extended chord each one is equivalent to, and the actual strings to pluck.

The notation

Polychords are written as upper triad over lower triad, separated by a horizontal bar. The upper chord is the right hand (treble), the lower is the left hand (bass).

2̂
─────
1̂

In plain text — where vertical stacking isn’t possible — the convention here is a tilde: 2̂~1̂. This distinguishes a polychord from a slash chord (2̂/1̂), which is a single chord with a specified bass note.

Why two hands?

On the harp, polychords let each hand stay in a comfortable triadic shape while producing sonorities too dense for one hand alone. 2̂~1̂ — a supertonic triad over the tonic triad — is just 1̂Δ9, but voiced so the left hand pedals a familiar tonic shape while the right hand plays the upper tensions.

Every polychord here is fully diatonic: every note is in the home scale, no levers or pedals to flip. Pick a key on the keyboard below and the strings update for that tuning.
