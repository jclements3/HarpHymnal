# Acoustic Samples

Shape combinations chosen for acoustic reasons rather than harmonic
function. Each sample exploits one of the harp's physical strengths:
simple frequency ratios between fundamentals, overtone alignment between
strings, sympathetic resonance from undamped neighbors, or the long
decay envelope.

Standard header for any sample below (transpose by changing `Tonic:` and
`Pedals:`):

```
Title:  <sample name>
Number: <NNN>
Tonic:  C
Pedals: ⠩⠹⠡⠒
---
<row of shapes>
```

The pedal cell `⠩⠹⠡⠒` is C major (all naturals). For drill use the
pedal line and `Number:` may be omitted; the player picks an octave and
key.

The samples are pure shape-setup sequences. Rhythm and articulation are
the player’s interpretation — the grid below tells the hand what to set,
not when to strike.

-----

## 1 · Wide-open low + stacked fifths above

**1. Open fifths tower** — `1x^1c5`
LH-side reach: degree 1 in octave 1, then 12th up (`c`), then a 5th
above that. Bottom dyad is a 1:4 ratio (two octaves), upper finger adds
a fifth — overtones of the bottom string include both upper notes
explicitly. Maximum overtone alignment.

**2. Stacked fifths through registers** — `1x^155 +2x^155`
Open-fifth triad (intervals 5+5 — root, 5th, 9th) climbed an octave.
The 3:2 stack is the simplest non-octave ratio; restamping at +2x
crosses into a register where adjacent strings start to share partials
with the bottom string. The whole soundboard wakes up.

**3. Lydian fifths** — `4x^155`
Same shape rooted on degree 4 (Lydian column). The #4 in upper voices
against tonic-feeling open fifths gives the floating quality without
breaking the simple-ratio frame.

## 2 · Octave-doubled bass with triad above

**4. Bass octave + triad two octaves up** — `1L8 3R^135`
LH spans an octave on the bass note (1:2 ratio); RH places a closed
triad two octaves above. Bass fuses into a single fat fundamental; the
upper triad sits in a register where its own intervals stay clear.

**5. Two-octave bass + open triad** — `1L8 3R^155`
Same bass setup, RH switches to the open-fifth triad. The `5` on top
puts the high voice on a partial already vibrating in the bass — direct
sympathetic excitation.

**6. Three-octave reach** — `1L^1 4R^135`
Single bass note (no doubling), triad three octaves up. Wide separation;
the bass acts as a drone whose decay carries the upper triad's
arrival. Best when the upper triad enters slightly after the bass.

## 3 · Stacked fourths through the middle register

**7. Quartal triad** — `3x^144`
Two stacked 4ths (root, 4th, 7th in interval terms — C, F, B♭ if rooted
on C Mixolydian; C, F, B in Ionian). The 4:3 ratio is simple but
ambiguous about which note is bottom. Floating, open color.

**8. Quartal climb** — `1x^144&3`
Same shape octave-stamped four times across the harp. Each successive
shape shares its bottom note with the top of the prior — partial
alignment carries the climb.

**9. Lydian quartal** — `4x^444`
Three stacked 4ths rooted on degree 4. Pure Lydian color; in C this is
F-B-E-A (all natural, all in the diatonic frame). The triple-4 stack
is mode-conditional — only sings in Lydian.

## 4 · Drone bass + sparse high pings

These use the **separate-shapes** form: a held LH single note followed
by a sequence of RH single-finger or small-shape pings above. The LH
shape is set first and the RH shapes change above it.

**10. Tonic drone + scale degrees above** — `1L^1 5R^1 5R^3 5R^5 5R^7 5R8`
LH on degree 1 in octave 1 (held). RH plucks single notes high up:
1, 3, 5, 7, 8 — each one a partial of the LH drone. Maximum
sympathetic ping; every RH note is already vibrating in the LH string
when struck.

**11. Drone + open dyad pings** — `1L^1 5R^13 5R^15 5R^18`
Same LH drone; RH plucks small dyads on degrees 1+3, 1+5, 1+8 high up.
Each RH dyad reinforces a different region of the LH overtone series.

**12. Drone + Lydian color pings** — `1L^1 5R^4 5R^6 5R8`
LH drone on Ionian root, RH pings on the Lydian-flavored upper degrees
(#4 against the natural tonic). Sympathetic resonance still happens
because all pitches are diatonic, but the upper voices add color the
overtone series doesn't strictly contain.

## 5 · Top-octave bell clusters

In the top octave, fundamentals are high enough that small-interval
clusters sound bell-like rather than muddy. Beating frequencies move
into the brightness range.

**13. Top-octave cluster triad** — `7x^123`
Degrees 1, 2, 4 packed into octave 7 (intervals 2+3). In octave 1 this
would sound muddy; up here it sounds like wind chimes.

**14. Top-octave second-stack** — `7x^122`
Three adjacent degrees in octave 7. Pure cluster — would be unusable in
the bass but rings cleanly at the top of the harp.

**15. Top-octave stepwise tetrad** — `7x^1222`
Four adjacent degrees. Heavy stepwise penalty in the aesthetic scoring
table generally, but the rule reverses in the top octave because the
beating becomes timbral, not rhythmic.

## 6 · Overtone-series voicing

The first six harmonics of a fundamental are: 1 (root), 8va (octave),
12 (octave + 5th), 15 (two octaves), 17 (two octaves + major 3rd),
19 (two octaves + 5th). Stacking these explicitly produces a chord
that's already physically present in the bottom string.

**16. Bottom four harmonics** — `1L^1 3R^155`
LH on tonic in octave 1 (the fundamental). RH plays root-5th-9th two
octaves up — the 4th, 5th, and 6th harmonics of the LH string.
Everything you play is already vibrating sympathetically; the explicit
strike just amplifies it.

**17. Five-harmonic stack across both hands** — `1x^1888,55`
Two-hand 6-finger tower: LH triple-octave climb (root, 8va, 15ma), RH
adds a 5th and another 5th above. Nearly the entire low-end overtone
series rendered as a single setable shape. Held; let it bloom.

**18. Compressed harmonic-series shape** — `1x^1853`
Single one-hand tetrad: root, 8va, 12 (8va+5th), 14 (8va+7th).
Intervals 8+5+3 — an "open" tetrad whose digits literally trace the
overtone series. `8` and `5` are large intervals, well within thumb
reach.

## 7 · Octave-doubled with chorusing

Two strings exactly an octave apart, struck with slight delay, produce
chorusing as the decay envelopes interleave. The harp's slow decay
makes this work where it wouldn't on a piano.

**19. Octave dyad** — `1x^18`
Single shape, two strings an octave apart. Player rolls slowly or
strikes in succession — the encoding doesn't enforce timing.

**20. Octave-stamped octaves** — `1x^18&3`
Octave dyad climbed four times — covers four octaves in ten plucked
strings. Each iteration shares its bottom string with the prior
iteration's top, so the climb is acoustically continuous.

**21. Two-hand octave wash** — `1L^18 3R^18`
LH on octave dyad in oct 1, RH on octave dyad in oct 3. Four strings
total, all in 1:2:4:8 ratios — perfect partial alignment, maximum
fusion.
